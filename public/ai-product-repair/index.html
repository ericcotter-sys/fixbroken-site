One break, documented.

Example content. Product details invented for illustration.

severity: high dimension: trust effort: 2–3 days owner: design + eng sample — invented

Break: the meeting summary appears instantly, with no indication of what it read or skipped.

Evidence. In the first-run flow, the summary renders in under a second with no source markers. Three of the five bullet points reference the transcript; two do not. A new user has no way to tell which is which, so they re-read the transcript to check — which takes longer than writing the summary themselves.

Likely cause. The model output is trusted end-to-end; the UI was designed for the happy path. Nothing in the interface admits uncertainty, so users assign the product one global credibility score — and one bad bullet sets it.

Recommended fix. Link each summary bullet to the transcript span it came from. Bullets without a source get a visible "unverified" state. Verification becomes a glance instead of a re-read, and the product earns trust per-claim instead of losing it globally.

Impact. Trust is the activation gate here. A user who checks one bullet and finds it wrong doesn't export, doesn't invite the team, and doesn't come back. Every growth experiment on the roadmap sits downstream of this fix.

Do not fix yet. The model. Output quality is not the bottleneck — verifiability is. Skip the fine-tuning sprint until users can see sources.

seven_day_plan (excerpt)
day 1–2 · source links behind a feature flag, transcript spans indexed
day 3–4 · "unverified" state for unsourced bullets, click tracking on both
day 5–7 · release to new signups, watch export rate against last cohort

// before you ask

The four questions everyone has.

The product isn't public yet. Can you still review it?

Yes — a staging link, a test account, or a recorded walkthrough all work. Drop it in the artifacts field and note how to get in.

What if the product is in better shape than we think?

Then the plan is short and says so. Severity ratings are honest in both directions, and the "do not fix yet" list keeps your roadmap from inventing work. You're paying for the call, not for page count.

Do you build the fixes?

Not as part of the teardown. The plan is written so your own team can run it — fields, owners, and a 7-day order of operations. If you want hands-on help afterward, that's a separate conversation, and it starts from the plan instead of a discovery phase.

Who sees what I submit?

The operator who writes your plan. Intakes are never published, and client work is never named publicly — the work page is fully redacted for the same reason.
